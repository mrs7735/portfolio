Harvest Now, Decrypt Later: The Quantum Cryptography Threat in 2026

DATE:2026.04.12
READ:21 MIN READ
AUTHOR:CYBER_OPERATIVE
Post-QuantumCryptographyHNDLQuantum ComputingNISTML-KEM2026

Most cybersecurity threats give defenders time to react. Harvest Now, Decrypt Later does not. Adversaries are intercepting and stockpiling encrypted data today with the intent of decrypting it years from now, once cryptographically relevant quantum computers exist. Every VPN session, TLS handshake, and encrypted email you rely on in 2026 may become readable in 2032 or 2035 — and for data with long confidentiality requirements, that window is already closed. This post documents the quantum cryptographic threat, the post-quantum migration underway across the industry, and what it means for your security posture.

⚠ DEFENSIVE RESEARCH — This post documents a publicly discussed threat model. Sources: NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography standards (FIPS 203/204/205), Cloudflare's public post-quantum roadmap (April 2026), the G7 Cyber Expert Group statement (January 2026), the World Economic Forum Global Cybersecurity Outlook 2026, and published research from Apple (PQ3), Signal (PQXDH), Thales, and Microchip Technology.

The Threat Model: Why 2026 Is a Turning Point

Classical public-key cryptography — RSA, elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman, ECDSA — derives its security from mathematical problems that are computationally infeasible to solve on classical computers. Factoring a 2048-bit RSA modulus would take conventional hardware longer than the age of the universe. A sufficiently large quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could do it in hours. That is the quantum threat in one sentence.

As of 2026 no such quantum computer exists publicly. The threat is not that your encryption breaks today. The threat is that network traffic captured today can be decrypted once the hardware catches up. Intelligence agencies with the capability to capture traffic at scale have strong incentives to stockpile encrypted communications. Cloudflare's published roadmap and the Thales 2026 Data Threat Report cite 61–87% of surveyed security leaders naming harvest-now-decrypt-later as a leading concern.

1. How Harvest Now, Decrypt Later Actually Works

The attack pattern has three phases separated by years or decades. Each phase is mechanically unremarkable — the novelty is the timeline.

# Phase 1 (2020–2026): Passive interception and storage
#
# Adversary with backbone network access captures TLS traffic
# They cannot read it, but they store the ciphertext
# Storage cost for encrypted traffic:
#   1 GB of TLS ciphertext  ≈ $0.02 on cheap archival storage
#   Capturing all traffic to a single organisation for one year
#   might generate 10–100 TB ≈ $200–$2,000 in storage
#
# Financially trivial for a nation-state, corporate competitor, or well-funded threat actor

# Phase 2 (ongoing): Pattern-of-life analysis on metadata
#
# Even without decryption, metadata is useful now
# Who connected to whom, when, how often, from where
# TLS SNI reveals which sites were visited (the hostname is in plaintext)
# Traffic volumes reveal when meetings happen, when releases are imminent

# Phase 3 (2030+, estimated): Bulk decryption once quantum hardware arrives
#
# When a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) becomes available
# The stored ciphertext is replayed through Shor's algorithm
# Every TLS 1.2/1.3 session using RSA or ECDHE key exchange is readable
# Emails encrypted with S/MIME or PGP using RSA keys become readable
# VPN tunnels using IKEv2 with classical Diffie-Hellman become readable

# Key insight: the data confidentiality window is:
# (year data was encrypted) + (years until CRQC exists)
# If you encrypted something in 2020 and a CRQC exists in 2032,
# that data had 12 years of confidentiality — regardless of how strong
# your encryption was against classical attackers.

2. Mosca's Theorem: The Risk Framework

Dr. Michele Mosca of the University of Waterloo formulated the risk model that now drives every quantum migration timeline. The question is not whether quantum computers will arrive. The question is whether your migration will finish before they do.

# Mosca's theorem (the X + Y > Z rule):
#
# X = years required to migrate your systems to post-quantum cryptography
# Y = years your data must remain confidential
# Z = years until a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists
#
# If X + Y > Z, you are already too late — migrate with urgency
# If X + Y < Z, you have some breathing room
#
# Typical enterprise values in 2026:
# X = 5 to 10 years (cryptographic infrastructure migration is slow)
# Y = 5 to 50 years (depends on data type — see below)
# Z = 5 to 15 years (estimates vary by source)
#
# Worked example: healthcare records
# X = 7 years (typical enterprise migration)
# Y = 25 years (patient privacy requirements under GDPR/HIPAA)
# Z = 10 years (conservative CRQC estimate)
# X + Y = 32 > Z = 10 → migration should have started years ago

# Data sensitivity windows by industry:
# Banking / trade secrets:        10–20 years
# Healthcare (patient records):   20–50 years
# National security / classified: 25–75 years
# Personal communications:        2–10 years (most fade in value)
# Source code / IP:               5–20 years
# Cryptographic identity:         5–30 years (certs, long-lived keys)

3. What NIST Standardised — The Post-Quantum Toolkit

In August 2024 NIST finalised the first three post-quantum cryptographic standards after an eight-year public competition. These are the algorithms that will replace RSA and ECC across the internet. Understanding what each does is essential for planning a migration.

# NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography standards (published August 2024):
#
# FIPS 203 — ML-KEM (Module-Lattice-based Key-Encapsulation Mechanism)
#   Previously known as: CRYSTALS-Kyber
#   Replaces: RSA key transport, ECDH key agreement
#   Use for: TLS key exchange, VPN tunnel setup, hybrid encryption
#   Parameter sets: ML-KEM-512, ML-KEM-768, ML-KEM-1024
#   Public key size: 800 / 1184 / 1568 bytes
#   Ciphertext size: 768 / 1088 / 1568 bytes
#   Security level: roughly equivalent to AES-128 / AES-192 / AES-256

# FIPS 204 — ML-DSA (Module-Lattice-based Digital Signature Algorithm)
#   Previously known as: CRYSTALS-Dilithium
#   Replaces: RSA signatures, ECDSA
#   Use for: code signing, document signing, TLS certificates
#   Parameter sets: ML-DSA-44, ML-DSA-65, ML-DSA-87
#   Signature size: 2420 / 3309 / 4627 bytes (vs ~256 bytes for ECDSA-P256)
#   The signature size is the biggest operational challenge

# FIPS 205 — SLH-DSA (Stateless Hash-based Digital Signature Algorithm)
#   Previously known as: SPHINCS+
#   Backup signature algorithm based on hash functions only
#   Use for: scenarios where ML-DSA's security assumptions are a concern
#   Slower and larger than ML-DSA but built on different math
#   Recommended for long-term root-of-trust signatures

# Upcoming (Standardisation in progress):
# FIPS 206 — FN-DSA (FALCON-based) — smaller signatures than ML-DSA
# HQC — alternative KEM based on code-based cryptography (backup for ML-KEM)

4. What's Actually Deployed in 2026

Post-quantum cryptography is no longer theoretical. Major platforms have already shipped it for key exchange, though authentication is lagging. Here's what was deployed as of early 2026 according to public vendor announcements.

# Key exchange (encryption): widely deployed in hybrid mode
# Hybrid mode = classical + PQ in parallel, secure as long as EITHER is unbroken
#
# Web browsers:
#   Chrome: X25519Kyber768 hybrid by default since Chrome 124 (April 2024)
#   Firefox: X25519Kyber768 hybrid behind flag, on by default in recent versions
#   Edge: same as Chrome (Chromium base)
# Connecting to Cloudflare, Google, or Cloudflare-protected sites?
# Your TLS handshake is probably already using hybrid post-quantum.
#
# Messaging:
#   Signal: PQXDH key agreement since September 2023
#   iMessage: PQ3 (Apple's hybrid protocol) since iOS 17.4 (March 2024)
#     — adds ongoing rekeying with post-quantum KEM
#   WhatsApp: no PQ key exchange in general deployment as of early 2026
#
# VPN:
#   Mullvad: WireGuard with ML-KEM hybrid since 2024
#   ProtonVPN: ML-KEM hybrid in OpenVPN and WireGuard
#   NordVPN: ML-KEM hybrid rolling out 2025–2026
#   Corporate VPNs (Palo Alto, Fortinet, Cisco): varying PQ support
#
# SSH:
#   OpenSSH 9.0+ supports sntrup761x25519-sha512 (hybrid KEM)
#   OpenSSH 9.9+ (Sept 2024) adds mlkem768x25519-sha256 per NIST standard
#
# Cloudflare: over 65% of human traffic uses post-quantum encryption
# Target: full post-quantum authentication by 2029 (per their April 2026 roadmap)

# Authentication (signatures): mostly NOT yet deployed
# This is the next frontier — TLS certificates still use RSA/ECDSA
# Migration is slower because every CA, every client, every server must update
# Cloudflare and Google publicly prioritising this in 2026–2029

5. The Migration Path: What Organisations Should Do Now

The EU has mandated that Member States develop post-quantum migration plans by 31 December 2026, with critical infrastructure secure by 2030. The G7 Cyber Expert Group issued a coordinated roadmap statement in January 2026. This is no longer an optional upgrade — for any organisation operating in regulated sectors, it is a compliance trajectory with hard deadlines.

# The migration in five phases:
#
# Phase 1: Cryptographic inventory (6–12 months)
# You cannot migrate what you cannot see
# Document every use of public-key cryptography across the organisation:
#   - TLS endpoints and their key exchange algorithms
#   - SSH host keys and user keys
#   - Code signing certificates
#   - Document signing and PKI infrastructure
#   - VPN configurations
#   - IoT device firmware update signatures
#   - Hardware security modules and their supported algorithms
#   - Third-party services that use cryptography on your behalf

# Tools for cryptographic inventory:
# OpenSSL command line to inspect TLS endpoints:
openssl s_client -connect target.com:443 -tls1_3 2>&1 | grep -iE "protocol|cipher|kex"

# Nmap NSE scripts for bulk enumeration:
nmap --script ssl-enum-ciphers -p 443 target.com
nmap --script ssh2-enum-algos -p 22 target.com

# Commercial tools:
# PQShield, InfoSec Global, Sandbox AQ, IBM Quantum Safe — enterprise inventory tooling

# Phase 2: Risk prioritisation (3–6 months)
# Rank cryptographic assets by:
#   data sensitivity × confidentiality window × exposure likelihood
# Long-confidentiality data on public networks = highest priority

# Phase 3: Pilot deployment (6–12 months)
# Roll out hybrid PQ/classical cryptography in non-critical systems first
# Test interoperability, performance impact, and operational procedures
# Hybrid is the correct choice during transition — if PQ algorithms have
# an undiscovered weakness, classical algorithms still protect the data

# Phase 4: Production migration (2–5 years)
# Replace classical-only cryptography across all systems
# Update certificate authorities, PKI, code signing infrastructure
# Upgrade firmware on IoT devices (the hardest step — many can't be updated)

# Phase 5: Decommissioning classical algorithms (1–3 years)
# Turn off legacy cryptography to prevent downgrade attacks
# This is when the migration is actually complete
# Most organisations will still be in Phase 3–4 by 2030

6. Crypto-Agility: The Architectural Prerequisite

The lesson from the PQC transition is not just about quantum computers. It is that cryptographic algorithms will continue to need replacement. Crypto-agility — the architectural ability to swap cryptographic primitives without rebuilding the system — is now a design requirement for any system expected to operate for more than five years.

# Crypto-agility principles:
#
# 1. Algorithm identifiers in data structures, not hardcoded
#    Bad:  always verify with ECDSA-P256
#    Good: read the signature algorithm from the structure and dispatch dynamically
#
# 2. Abstract cryptographic operations behind an interface
#    Your application code should never call RSA or ECDSA directly
#    It should call a sign() / verify() / encrypt() / decrypt() interface
#    The implementation can then be swapped without changing application code
#
# 3. Library choice matters
#    Use libraries that expose algorithm agility:
#    - liboqs (Open Quantum Safe) — PQ algorithms with classical fallback
#    - Bouncy Castle (Java/C#) — comprehensive algorithm support
#    - BoringSSL / OpenSSL 3+ — modern algorithm registration APIs
#
# 4. Certificate chains with algorithm diversity
#    Issue hybrid certificates containing both classical and PQ signatures
#    IETF draft: composite signatures combine multiple algorithms into one cert
#
# 5. Protocol versioning that allows algorithm negotiation
#    TLS 1.3 already does this correctly — algorithms negotiated per-connection
#    Proprietary protocols often don't — and then become migration nightmares

# Validation: stress-test your agility
# Can you deprecate SHA-1 tomorrow? (You should have already)
# Can you rotate from ECDSA-P256 to ML-DSA-65 in a quarter? (You should be able to)
# How many services would break if you disabled RSA today? (That number should be small)

7. What This Means for You Right Now

For personal communications and browsing, most major services have already shipped hybrid PQ key exchange. Signal, iMessage, Mullvad, and Cloudflare-protected websites are already protected. For enterprise systems, the work is just beginning. Three concrete actions for security teams in 2026:

# Action 1: Inventory now, regardless of migration timing
# You will need this inventory whether you migrate this year or in 2030
# Starting without inventory guarantees missed systems and failed migration

# Action 2: Treat new cryptographic deployments as hybrid from day one
# Every new TLS endpoint, every new signing key, every new protocol:
# implement with crypto-agility and prefer hybrid PQ/classical modes
# This prevents you from adding to the migration debt

# Action 3: Track your CA/PKI provider's PQC roadmap
# Enterprise certificate authorities (DigiCert, Sectigo, Entrust) have
# public PQC timelines. Your internal PKI infrastructure will need to
# follow their lead. If your PKI vendor has no published roadmap,
# that is a procurement red flag in 2026.

# For personal use — verify you are protected for free:
# Chrome: chrome://flags/#post-quantum-kex (should be enabled by default)
# Test site: https://pq.cloudflareresearch.com (shows if your browser
#           negotiated a PQ handshake)
# Signal: automatic, no configuration required (since 2023)
# iMessage: iOS 17.4+ and macOS 14.4+ (enable by updating, then wait 30 days
#           for PQ3 to complete its progressive rollout for your account)
